Written byElena Marçal

A house that refuses
to be photographed.

There is a small house on the cliffs above Cascais that has been the subject of more failed photographs than perhaps any other building in Portugal. It was finished in 1971. The architect, who is now ninety-three and lives in the next valley, has politely declined every interview request for thirty years. The owners have changed three times. Each, in turn, has invited a photographer; each photographer has left, in the end, defeated.

The problem is not the light. The light, in fact, is celebrated. The problem is that the house was designed to be walked through at the pace of a slow breath — and a photograph, however generous, is always taken at the speed of an aperture. What the lens captures is geometry. What the house gives is duration.

I spent four mornings there this April, at the invitation of its current owner, a textile designer who asked not to be named. She offered me coffee, a chair near the eastern window, and the only condition that I bring no camera. "You can write what you remember," she said. "That is the only honest record this place permits."

"Some rooms cannot be reproduced. They can only be visited, and then forgotten in the particular way they ask to be forgotten." — Elena Marçal, on the unphotographable house

By the second morning I had stopped trying to describe the rooms and had started, instead, to describe the time between them — the seven seconds it takes to cross from the library to the south-facing courtyard, the way the limestone underfoot grows warm in a sequence that you only notice on the third or fourth passage. This is, I think, what the architect understood and what no photograph has ever managed to hold: that a house can be a clock, and that the most luxurious thing it can offer is its own particular speed.
